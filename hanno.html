The Commission

Not a trip through West Africa. An expedition in Hanno's wake.

Around 500 BC a Carthaginian admiral named Hanno took a fleet of sixty ships out through the Pillars of Hercules and turned south, down a coast no Mediterranean power had charted. The account he left — the Periplus of Hanno — is the oldest first-hand record of exploration that survives. Colonies founded on the Atlantic shore. A trading island called Cerne. A river of crocodiles and hippopotami. A bay where drums sounded in the dark. And, at the limit of the voyage, a mountain on fire that the Carthaginians called Theon Ochema — the Chariot of the Gods.

This is not a tour that visits those countries. It is an expedition that follows the strongest historical reading of the text itself — Carthage, the Strait, the Moroccan colonies, Lixus, the Cerne region, Mauritania, the great river-mouths of Senegal, and only as far south as the evidence and the ground genuinely allow. You travel the way the journey deserves to be travelled: by local boat and fishing pirogue, by desert train and 4×4, on foot and on water. Authenticity before luxury. The expedition, not the highlight reel.

The Route

Seven chapters, north to south

Each chapter is anchored to a passage of the Periplus and to a place you can still stand in. Where the scholarship is solid, we say so. Where it is inspired guesswork, we say that too.

✦ITunisia · the Strait
Chapter I

Carthage & the Pillars

Tunisia · the Strait of Gibraltar

Begin where the fleet did: the circular military harbour of Carthage, the Tophet, the museum on Byrsa Hill. Then the one concession the map demands — there is no land route west through Algeria, so we fly to Tangier and pick the coast back up. At Cap Spartel, where the Mediterranean pours into the Atlantic, you cross the Strait itself by boat and turn south. The Pillars of Hercules behind you. Open ocean ahead.

You stand on it: the Punic ports of Carthage — genuine, excavated, Hanno's own harbour.
✦IIAtlantic Morocco
Chapter II

The Lost Colonies

Atlantic Morocco

The Periplus lists a string of cities founded in a single day along this shore. We travel it by coastal rail and private road: the genuinely Phoenician ruins of Lixus above the Loukkos river; Roman-Punic Sala at Rabat; and the island of Mogador off Essaouira, a confirmed Phoenician dye-works you watch from the ramparts. At Safi and Cape Beddouza — a strong candidate for Hanno's Cape Soloeis — you ride out with the dawn sardine fleet.

You stand on it: Lixus and Mogador — two sites with confirmed Phoenician material older than the voyage itself.
✦IIIThe Saharan Atlantic
Chapter III

The Edge of the Map

The Saharan Atlantic · Cerne

South of Agadir the road thins and the Sahara meets the sea. We run the coastal highway down through Tarfaya and Laâyoune to Dakhla, on its long lagoon. Out in the bay lies Herné Island — the leading candidate for Cerne, the trading post at the far edge of Hanno's known world. We take a boat to it at low tide. No one can prove a Carthaginian stood here. Standing here yourself, you understand why they thought one did.

Honest note: Cerne is a scholarly best-guess, not a certainty — and the finest kind of place to argue about at a campfire.
✦IVMauritania
Chapter IV

The River of Crocodiles

Mauritania

Our home leg, and the heart of the journey. Cross into Mauritania and ride one of the longest trains on earth across the desert, on the iron ore. Reach the Banc d'Arguin, where the Imraguen still fish from lateen-sailed pirogues exactly as the coast's people have for millennia — and where, in winter, dolphins drive the mullet into their nets. Camp under the cleanest sky you will ever see. End at the Nouakchott fish market at dawn.

The signature scene: sailing with the Imraguen — a living maritime tradition, and the closest you will come to Hanno's own decks.
✦VSenegal · Gambia · Bijagós
Chapter V

The Western Horn

Senegal · The Gambia · the Bijagós

Now the great river-mouths the text describes. Saint-Louis and the Langue de Barbarie at the mouth of the Senegal — a strong reading of Hanno's "Chretes." Pirogue through the Saloum delta and down to the Casamance by water, avoiding the roads. The River Gambia, with its hippos and crocodiles. And finally the labyrinth of the Bijagós islands — a candidate for the "Horn of the West," reached by local boat, where the drums the Carthaginians heard in the dark still sound.

You stand on it: the Senegal and Gambia river-mouths — the crocodile-and-hippo country the Periplus actually names.
✦VIThe Guinea Coast
Chapter VI

The Fiery Coast Conditional Leg

Guinea · Sierra Leone · Liberia · Côte d'Ivoire

The expedition's hardest overland stretch — and the one we plan around live conditions, not romance. Long days on rough roads, real border friction, a flight where the route demands it. Freetown's Lion Mountains, the Banana Islands, the old colonial coast. No coastal sailing here: the Gulf of Guinea is the world's piracy capital and that line is absolute. We run this leg only when the ground says yes, and we tell you plainly when it does not.

Honest note: a grind, not a cruise — included for the completists, scoped to the security picture at the time of travel.
✦VIIto the Chariot of the Gods
Chapter VII

The Chariot of the Gods Terminus · Conditional

Ghana · Benin · Cameroon · Gabon

The home straight runs through our own ground — Elmina and Cape Coast, the Vodun shrines of Ouidah, the stilt-city of Ganvié. Then the terminus the maximalist reading points to: Mount Cameroon, the only live volcano on this whole coast, almost certainly Hanno's burning Theon Ochema. We bring you to its shoulder as the journey's final act. The summit climb itself sits in a conflict zone today and is deferred — held until it can be done safely. A symbolic arrival now; the ascent when the mountain is open again.

Honest note: Mt Cameroon is the best match in all the scholarship for the Chariot of the Gods — and currently a Do-Not-Travel zone. We will not sell you the climb until it is safe. That is the promise.

How We Handle Risk

The expedition you can actually take — and the parts we won't fake.

You asked for historical faithfulness over comfort, and for honesty over a hard sell. So here is exactly where the line sits. This is the same judgement we apply to every Waypoint expedition: we go to difficult places, and we are straight about what that means.

What we will do

  • Build the Carthage-to-Bijagós core in full — the strongest, safest, most authentic two-thirds of the route.
  • Put you on the iron-ore train and under sail with the Imraguen, on our own permits.
  • Move you by local boat, bush taxi, rail and 4×4 — the expedition's true texture.
  • Carry satellite comms, medical-evacuation cover and 24/7 ground support the entire way.

What we won't do

  • Sail the Nigerian coast. The Gulf of Guinea piracy risk makes it uninsurable — we fly over it.
  • Sell you a Mount Cameroon summit today. Its region is under a Do-Not-Travel advisory; the climb is deferred.
  • Pretend the Guinea-coast leg is smooth. It is a grind, scoped to live conditions.
  • Quote a final figure before our fixers confirm. The prices here are honest planning ranges.
