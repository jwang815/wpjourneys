The Hanno Expedition

Feasibility, Fixers & Costing — Carthage to the Gulf of Guinea
Prospect: "spalind" (IG) · Solo traveller · Winter window · Budget flexible, authenticity over luxury · Prepared 28 June 2026
Bottom line up front

Yes — for roughly four-fifths of the route. Build it in phases; defer the climax.

This is a real, deliverable expedition from Carthage to Ghana/Benin, and unusually for us, three of the hardest legs sit on ground we already operate (Tunisia, Mauritania, West Africa). The northern two-thirds — Tunisia → Morocco → Western Sahara → Mauritania → Senegal/Bijagós — is the strongest: safe enough in winter, genuinely authentic, and home to the only two confirmed Punic sites on the whole route.

The one piece we cannot sell responsibly in 2026 is the maximalist terminus. Mount Cameroon — the "Chariot of the Gods" — is under a Do-Not-Travel advisory with an active insurgency. The Nigerian coast is out on piracy grounds. Recommendation: contract the Core (≈45 days) now, price the southern phases as conditional add-ons, and hold the summit until the ground allows.

1 · The concept, and why it holds up

The prospect doesn't want a West Africa trip — he wants a recreation of Hanno the Navigator's voyage (the Periplus of Hanno, c. 500 BC), following the strongest historical reading of the text rather than modern borders, travelled rough: overland, local boats, fishing pirogues, hiking, local guides. That instinct is sound. The route below is anchored to the text and, critically, to places that still physically exist.

Honest framing for the client: everything south of Lixus is scholarly inference on a deliberately obscured text (a single Byzantine manuscript of a lost Punic original). Lead authenticity claims with Lixus and Mogador; present Cerne, the Horn of the West and the Chariot of the Gods as "the leading hypothesis," never as fact. The prospect explicitly values that honesty — it's a selling point, not a weakness.

The one hard "no": the Chariot of the Gods. Mt Cameroon sits in the Southwest Region, the epicentre of the Anglophone crisis — Do-Not-Travel (US L4) / advise-against-all-travel (UK), with documented 2025–26 attacks, IEDs and abductions. No reputable operator is running the climb for Westerners right now. Selling it would be indefensible. Keep it in the story as the terminus; deliver it as a symbolic fly-in to Douala/Gabon, and bank the actual ascent as a future Phase II once a ceasefire holds.

3 · The Waypoint advantage — three legs already in-house

This is the single biggest reason to take the commission, and the biggest margin lever. Three of the route's hardest segments are existing Waypoint expeditions, which means existing fixers, vehicles, accommodation contracts and — crucially — permits:

  • Tunisia (Carthage) — our Tunisia & Algeria expedition already covers Carthage. The departure leg runs at in-house cost.
  • Mauritania (the Iron-Ore Train) — our Mauritania expedition already holds the SNIM permit relationship needed to ride the ore wagons (now closed to independent travellers) and to reach the Banc d'Arguin. This de-risks the crux leg entirely.
  • Ghana · Togo · Benin — our West Africa expedition is home ground (Elmina, Ouidah, Ganvié), run at in-house rates with established drivers and fixers.

Net effect: three legs carry no third-party DMC markup, lifting blended margin and shortening the quote-gathering cycle. The cost model already reflects in-house pricing on these rows.

7 · Recommendation & next steps

  • Sell the Core first. Carthage→Bijagós (≈45 days, from ~$60k at 50% margin) is the strongest, safest, fully-deliverable product and stands alone. Quote it at 50–55% given the build complexity and our in-house leverage on three legs.
  • Offer Grand & Full as phased add-ons, explicitly conditional on live security. This matches our modular house style and the prospect's appetite without over-promising.
  • Defer the Mt Cameroon summit. Keep it as the narrative terminus; deliver a symbolic fly-in to Douala/Gabon now; bank the ascent as Phase II when the Anglophone ceasefire holds.
  • Firm the numbers: send quote requests to the third-party fixers in the tracker; the in-house legs (Tunisia, Mauritania, West Africa) we can price internally today. Re-pull every advisory at contract — Guinea-Bissau (coup), Cameroon, and Gabon are moving.
  • Confirm scope with the client: total duration he can commit to (the Full is ~10–11 weeks), and target travel month (winter is the only sane window for the Sahara coast).

8 · Confidence & sources

Costs are planning-grade estimates, not quotes; many operators price on request. Security and political conditions are volatile on this route (Guinea-Bissau Nov-2025 coup, Cameroon Anglophone crisis, Gabon post-coup transition) — re-verify before any commitment. Mauritania requires an e-visa secured in advance; the iron-ore ore-wagon now requires an operator with a SNIM permit.
